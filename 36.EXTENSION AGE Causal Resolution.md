This is a tricky but very solvable problem once we think in terms of **temporal isolation, causal windows, and narrative overlays**. Let me unpack this carefully and explore the options.

---

# 1️⃣ Core Problem

- You have **hierarchical narrative + spatial partitions**.
    
- Each partition has a **time abstraction** (Epic → World → months/years, down to Spotlight → seconds/minutes).
    
- **Multiple troupes** may operate in overlapping spatial/narrative partitions.
    
- Player decisions in one troupe can **conflict with the events experienced by another troupe** if their stories overlap in space and time.
    

This is exactly a **chronological concurrency problem**, akin to multi-user simulation or distributed game state in a dynamic world.

---

# 2️⃣ Your Proposed Solutions

### **Solution 1: Strict Isolation**

- Each troupe gets **its own temporal sandbox** within shared partitions.
    
- TroupeB cannot start until TroupeA completes all chronologically relevant events.
    
- Flags set by TroupeA are only “read” by the Fiction Constructor **after both troupes’ arcs resolve**.
    

**Pros:**

- Deterministic, easy to reason about.
    
- Prevents narrative conflict entirely.
    
- Simplifies world-state for each troupe.
    

**Cons:**

- Reduces emergent world feeling — troupes cannot overlap in the same world in real time.
    
- Less flexible for concurrent gameplay.
    

---

### **Solution 2: Guided Overlap / Temporal Conflict Resolution**

- TroupeB may enter partitions where TroupeA has already acted, but:
    
    - Certain flags can be **unset or masked** to prevent paradoxes.
        
    - Only “non-causality-critical” flags propagate to TroupeB.
        
    - Narrative construction steers troupeB away from critical intersections (e.g., they encounter ruins rather than live battles).
        

**Pros:**

- More emergent and concurrent world experience.
    
- Preserves dynamic story potential between troupes.
    

**Cons:**

- Complex: requires classification of flags by **causal importance**.
    
- Risk of players noticing inconsistencies if narrative guidance is imperfect.
    
- Requires more logic in Fiction Constructor and world-state controller.
    

---

# 3️⃣ Additional Solutions / Extensions

Beyond your two options, there are a few more approaches:

---

### **Solution 3: Temporal Sharding with Convergent Resolution**

- Each troupe progresses independently in **a local “chronology shard”**.
    
- Flags from one shard are only merged at **epoch boundaries** (e.g., end of campaign arc).
    
- Fiction Constructor can **retcon or reconcile narrative outcomes** between shards to prevent paradoxes.
    

**Pros:**

- Supports asynchronous multi-troupe play.
    
- Emergent events can still influence future arcs without breaking causality.
    

**Cons:**

- Requires careful merge logic to avoid inconsistent outcomes.
    
- Players may notice “revisions” if a merged world-state changes something they experienced.
    

---

### **Solution 4: Causal Window Simulation**

- For overlapping troupes, define a **causal window** (time buffer) during which certain flags are **frozen or provisional**.
    
- Player actions in troupeB may be simulated against the **snapshot of world-state** at that causal window.
    
- Only after troupeB completes, provisional flags are reconciled into canonical world-state.
    

**Pros:**

- Allows concurrency without risking paradoxes.
    
- Can selectively propagate causal effects after troupe completion.
    

**Cons:**

- Needs bookkeeping of provisional vs canonical flags.
    
- Might require the Fiction Constructor to resolve “what actually happened” for the next arc.
    

---

### **Solution 5: Multi-Threaded Narrative with Conflict Resolution**

- Each troupe effectively exists in a **parallel timeline** within the same world-state.
    
- When events converge (e.g., troupes meet the same NPCs or locations), the Fiction Constructor dynamically generates **conflict-aware narrative outcomes**:
    
    - Example: ruins left by TroupeA become the scene for TroupeB.
        
    - TroupeB cannot negate TroupeA’s canonical actions — instead, they experience consequences indirectly.
        

**Pros:**

- Preserves narrative concurrency.
    
- Emergent, living-world feeling.
    

**Cons:**

- Computationally and logically complex.
    
- May limit some player freedom to directly alter previous troupe outcomes.
    

---

# 4️⃣ Key Design Principles

1. **Flags should have metadata**:
    
    - `causal_importance` (critical / non-critical)
        
    - `temporal_scope` (epoch, campaign, session)
        
    - `origin_troupe`
        
2. **Chronological partitioning scales naturally**:
    
    - Epic → World → months/years → less granular
        
    - Spotlight → sub-map → seconds/minutes → most granular
        
3. **Temporal isolation + selective merge** is usually safer than fully concurrent processing:
    
    - Prevents paradoxes and narrative inconsistencies.
        
    - Supports multi-troupe play with manageable complexity.
        
4. **Fiction Constructor guidance is essential**:
    
    - Directs troupes away from potential conflicts or generates narrative to reconcile overlapping flags.

- Use **flag metadata and temporal layers** to manage causality and reconcile outcomes.
    
- Background NPC events and player actions can still co-exist in the hybrid model, but the **Fiction Constructor orchestrates the narrative for each troupe within temporal partitions**.
